What is Panchakarma Treatment?

Panchakarma is a treatment program for the body, mind, and consciousness that cleanses and rejuvenates. It is based on Ayurvedic principles, every human is a unique phenomenon manifested through the five basic elements of Ether, Air, Fire, Water, and Earth.
The combination of these elements are three doshas (tridosha): Vata, Pitta, and Kapha, and their balance is unique to each individual. When this doshic balance is disturbed it creates disorder resulting in disease.
Panchakarma is done individually for each person with their specific constitution and specific disorder in mind, thus it requires close observation and supervision. Treatment starts with pre-purification Measures of Snehan and Svedana, and then cleansing methods – Shodanas, are applied.

Every individual constitution has its own unique balance of vata, pitta, and kapha (VPK) according to its own nature. This balance of VPK is the natural order. When this doshic balance is disturbed, it creates imbalance, which is disorder.
Health is order; disease is disorder. Within the body there is a constant interaction between order and disorder, thus once one understands the nature and structure of disorder, one can re-establish order. Ayurveda believes that order lies within disorder. Order is the state of health, as defined by Ayurveda Panchakarma.
This exists when the digestive fire (agni) is in a balanced condition; the bodily humors (vata, pitta, and kapha) are in equilibrium, the three waste products (urine, feces, and sweat) are produced and eliminated normally, the seven bodily tissues (rasa, rakta, mamsa, meda, asthi, majja and shukra/artava) are functioning normally, and the mind, senses, and consciousness are working harmoniously together.
When the balance of these systems is disturbed, the disease (disorder) process begins.

Yoga & Activity

Gentle exercises and breathing techniques to promote physical, mental, and emotional well-being.

Yoga & Activity

Yoga

* Yoga is an ancient practice originating from India that encompasses physical, mental, and spiritual disciplines. In Ayurveda, yoga is considered a vital component of a holistic approach to health.
* Yoga postures (asanas), breathing techniques (pranayama), and meditation are fundamental aspects of Ayurvedic practice. The practice of yoga is believed to balance the doshas (Vata, Pitta, and Kapha), improve circulation, enhance digestion, strengthen the immune system, and promote mental clarity and emotional balance.
* Different yoga poses and sequences are recommended based on an individual's unique constitution and imbalances. For example, calming and grounding poses may be suggested for someone with excess Vata, while cooling and soothing poses may be recommended for individuals with excess Pitta.

Pregnancy Yoga

* Pregnancy yoga is a gentle form of yoga tailored specifically for pregnant women. It typically focuses on poses and breathing techniques that help alleviate discomfort, reduce stress, and prepare the body for childbirth. It's important to practice under the guidance of a qualified instructor who understands the specific needs and limitations of pregnant women.

Physical Activity

* In Ayurveda, physical activity is seen as essential for maintaining good health and preventing disease. However, the type and intensity of physical activity recommended can vary based on an individual's constitution (Prakriti), current imbalances (Vikriti), age, season, and other factors.
* Ayurveda emphasizes the importance of moderation in physical activity. Overexertion or excessive exercise can lead to imbalances in the doshas and may cause harm rather than benefit.
* Activities such as walking, swimming, cycling, and gentle forms of exercise are often recommended as they promote circulation, strengthen the body, and help clear ama (toxins) from the system.
* Ayurveda also recognizes the importance of adapting physical activity to align with the rhythms of nature and the individual's energy levels. For example, vigorous exercise may be more suitable during the Kapha season (spring), while gentler activities may be preferable during the hot Pitta season (summer).
* In Ayurveda, both yoga and physical activity are considered integral components of a healthy lifestyle. When practiced mindfully and in accordance with one's unique constitution and needs, they can contribute to overall well-being, balance, and harmony of the body, mind, and spirit.
